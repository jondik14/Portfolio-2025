Foragecaster

Helping farmers make data-driven decisions about pasture management through predictive analytics and satellite imagery.

Role Product Designer
Timeline 2023-2024
Platform iOS & Android

The Problem

Australian farmers face unpredictable weather patterns and rising costs. Pasture management decisions—when to move livestock, when to destock, when to buy feed—are often made on gut instinct rather than data.

Foragecaster aims to change that by combining satellite imagery, weather data, and predictive models to give farmers actionable insights about their pastures.

"I can see my paddocks from space, but I still don't know if I should move my cattle next week or wait another month."

Understanding the Users

I spent time on farms across Queensland and NSW, shadowing farmers during their daily routines. Key observations:

  • Mobile-first by necessity: Farmers are rarely at a desk—they need information in the paddock
  • Glanceable data: Quick decisions happen frequently; deep analysis happens weekly
  • Trust in simplicity: Complex charts and graphs create skepticism, not confidence
  • Connectivity challenges: Many farms have poor or intermittent internet access

Design Principles

Based on research, I established three core principles for the app:

  1. Show, don't tell: Visual representations over numbers where possible
  2. Offline-first: Core functionality works without connectivity
  3. Progressive detail: Surface-level insights with depth available on demand

Interaction mapping and initial wireframes

Key Features

  • Paddock Health Dashboard: Color-coded overview of all paddocks with growth predictions
  • Feed Wedge Calculator: Visual tool showing feed availability vs. livestock requirements
  • Weather Integration: 14-day forecasts overlaid with pasture growth predictions
  • Historical Comparison: Compare current conditions to same time last year

Outcome

The app launched in beta with 50 pilot farms. Early feedback has been positive, with farmers reporting increased confidence in their pasture management decisions and reduced feed costs.

Designing for farmers taught me that the best technology often feels invisible—it enhances expertise rather than replacing it.
